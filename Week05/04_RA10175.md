# Punishable Acts under Cybercrime Prevention Act of 2012 (RA 10175)

Based on the Cybercrime Prevention Act of 2012 (R.A. No. 10175), the punishable acts are divided into three main categories.

## A. Offenses Against the Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability of Computer Data and Systems

### 1. Illegal Access

- Example 1: A jealous partner guesses or steals their significant other’s password to log into their private social media accounts and read their direct messages without authorization.

- Example 2: An employee logs into a restricted HR database containing the salary information of company executives without having the required legal right or clearance.

- Example 3: A hacker uses a vulnerability scanner to find an open port in a local school's server and gains access to the administrative backend.

### 2. Illegal Interception

- Example 1: A malicious actor sets up a fake, unencrypted public Wi-Fi network at a coffee shop and uses "packet sniffing" software to capture usernames and passwords being transmitted by unsuspecting customers.

- Example 2: An individual installs a wiretapping or data-sniffing program on a local business network to secretly record and copy private VOIP phone calls or data packets passing between office computers.

- Example 3: A tech-savvy individual intercepts a private, encrypted live video feed from a neighbor's smart home security camera system.

### 3. Data Interference

- Example 1: A disgruntled IT employee deletes critical, unrecoverable source code from the company's repository right before they quit.

- Example 2: A hacker infects a hospital's server with ransomware that scrambles and locks patient medical records, rendering them completely deteriorated and inaccessible until a ransom is paid.

- Example 3: Someone alters the code of a company website to systematically change client phone numbers and email addresses to random characters.

### 4. System Interference

- Example 1: An individual launches a Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attack against a government agency website, flooding it with millions of fake visits until the server crashes and legitimate citizens cannot access public services.

- Example 2: An attacker transmits a destructive computer worm through a corporate network that slows down computer processing to a complete crawl, completely halting the company’s daily operations.

- Example 3: A rogue actor floods a local utility provider's smart-grid control network with junk data packets, disrupting the system's ability to communicate with regional power stations.

### 5. Misuse of Devices

- Example 1: A software developer creates a custom "keylogger" program designed specifically to run invisibly and steal bank credentials, and then sells it on a dark web forum.

- Example 2: A person purchases and imports physical hardware devices ("skimmers") designed specifically to be attached to ATMs to illegally read and copy credit card magnetic strips.

- Example 3: Someone distributes a pre-packaged hacking toolkit online alongside instructions on how to use it to bypass password firewalls.

### 6. Cyber-squatting

- Example 1: Registering the domain name www.official-popularbrand-philippines.com before the actual brand can do so, with the intention of forcing them to buy it from you at an exorbitant price.

- Example 2: Registering a domain name using a famous local celebrity's exact name (e.g., www.famousname.com) to host gossip blogs or profit from ad revenue generated by their name recognition.

- Example 3: Buying up dozens of variations of a rising startup's name as domain extensions (.net, .org, .biz) in bad faith to prevent them from establishing a unified web presence unless they pay you off.

## B. Computer-Related Offenses

### 1. Computer-Related Forgery

- Example 1: A job applicant uses a digital image editor to alter the GPA and graduation year on a scanned PDF copy of a university transcript before uploading it to a job application portal.

- Example 2: An individual modifies a digital copy of a medical certificate or a positive COVID-19/laboratory test result to present it to their employer as a legitimate reason for paid sick leave.

- Example 3: Altering the digital signature block on an electronic contract to make it appear as though a business partner signed and agreed to a deal when they did not.

### 2. Computer-Related Fraud

- Example 1: An individual hacks into an e-commerce website's database and changes the price of a ₱60,000 television to ₱60 so they can purchase it at a fraction of the cost, causing economic damage to the store.

- Example 2: Altering the digital routing number database of a payroll system so that direct deposits meant for multiple employees are automatically diverted into the fraudster's personal bank account.

- Example 3: Manipulating the code of an online bidding or raffle website to ensure that a specific, pre-determined user account wins the cash prize.

### 3. Computer-Related Identity Theft

- Example 1: Creating a fake Facebook or Instagram account using a real person's photos, name, and biographical details, then messaging their friends to ask for emergency financial loans.

- Example 2: Acquiring someone's digital banking credentials and Personal Identifiable Information (PII) to apply for an online personal loan or credit card in their name without their consent.

- Example 3: Setting up a fake corporate email address that perfectly mimics a company executive's profile to issue authorized corporate directives to subordinates.

## C. Content-Related Offenses

### 1. Cybersex

- Example 1: Operating a commercial, subscription-based webcam service where performers are hired to engage in live, explicit sexual acts on camera for paid global subscribers.

- Example 2: An individual sets up a private chat room where they charge users a fee via digital wallets to view them engaging in real-time, interactive explicit sexual exhibitions.

- Example 3: A group of people establishing an online network that streams interactive adult entertainment services in exchange for cryptocurrencies or digital favors.

### 2. Child Pornography

- Example 1: Uploading folders containing child sexual abuse material (CSAM) onto a cloud storage provider and sharing the download links on online forums.

- Example 2: Utilizing a peer-to-peer messaging network or chat application to trade, download, or distribute media files depicting the exploitation or abuse of minors.

- Example 3: Hosting or maintaining an active database server on the internet dedicated to archiving and distributing illicit media involving children.

### 3. Unsolicited Commercial Communications (Spam)

- Example 1: A marketing company uses automated software to scrape thousands of email addresses off public forums and mass-emails them an advertisement for a cryptocurrency scheme without their prior consent or an "unsubscribe" option.

- Example 2: A retail store sends daily text blast advertisements about a new product to thousands of phone numbers without ever getting affirmative consent from those individuals to receive marketing texts.

- Example 3: An online lender floods a user’s instant messaging inbox with dozens of unsolicited loan advertisements without giving the recipient an opt-out mechanism.

### 4. Online Libel

- Example 1: Writing and publishing a public Facebook post falsely accusing a local restaurant owner of using spoiled ingredients and bribing health inspectors, damaging their business reputation out of spite.

- Example 2: Uploading a YouTube video making unverified, malicious claims that a public official is embezzling town funds, with the sole intent of destroying their public standing.

- Example 3: Posting a detailed thread on an online public forum containing fabrications about a private citizen's personal life, framing them as a criminal without any legal basis.
