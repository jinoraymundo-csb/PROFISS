# Week 07: A Framework for Making Ethical Decisions

## I. What is Ethics?

Ethics provides a set of standards to help us decide how we ought to act. It differs from religion (which focuses on broader or specific spiritual behaviors), law (which establishes rigid, universal guidelines), and morality (which ethics provides a rational basis for).

---

## II. Three Types of Ethical Theories

### A. The Consequentialist Framework

- **Focus**:
The future effects and outcomes of an action

- **Goal**:
To produce the most good and the least harm for everyone affected.

- **Approaches**:
Includes **Utilitarianism** (maximizing overall happiness), **Egoism** (maximizing self-interest), and the **Common Good** (contributing to communal life)

- **Pros/Cons**:
Pragmatic for large groups, but future consequences are hard to predict and it can justify bad means for good ends.

### B. The Duty Framework (Deontological)

- **Focus**:
Duties, rules, and obligations that exist prior to the situation.

- **Goal**:
To perform the correct action and treat people with equal dignity, regardless of the outcome.

- **Approaches**:
Includes **Duty-Based** (Kantian categorical imperatives), **Rights** (protecting human dignity), **Justice/Fairness** (treating people equally), and **Divine Command** (following God's will).

- **Pros/Cons**:
Creates consistent expectations for everyone, but can be cold, rigid, and struggles when two duties conflict.

### C. The Virtue Framework

- **Focus**:
Individual character traits and the kind of person one should be.

- **Goal**:
To develop good character and act as a fully virtuous person would.

- **Approaches**:
Includes **Virtue Ethics ** (building lifelong character and habits) and **Feminist Ethics** (emphasizing care and the experiences of marginalized groups).

- **Pros/Cons**:
Considers the whole of human experience, but makes it difficult to resolve specific disputes or establish concrete action rules.

---

## III. Step-by-Step Process for Ethical Decision-Making

When faced with a difficult choice, you can apply these frameworks using the following steps:

1. **Recognize the Ethical Issue**: Separate the moral questions from disputes over facts

2. **Consider the Parties involved**: Identify who will be harmed and who will benefit

3. **Gather Relevant Information**: Ensure you have all the facts before acting

4. **Formulate and Evaluate Alternatives**: Ask yourself which option does the most good (Utilitarian), respects rights (Rights), treats people fairly (Justice), serves the community (Common Good), or reflects good character (Virtue).

5. **Make a Decision**: Choose the option that best addresses the situation.

6. **Act and Reflect**: Implement the choice and look back later to evaluate the intended and unintended consequences.

## IV. A Scenario

> A large tech company discovers a security flaw in their software. Fixing it properly will take three weeks and require taking their service offline, costing clients millions in lost business. Patching it secretly while keeping services running minimizes immediate financial loss but leaves user data vulnerable to a breach for those three weeks.

| Ethical Framework | Core Focus |
| --- | --- |
| Consequentialist | The future outcomes and results of the action. |
| Duty (Deontological) | Pre-existing rules, obligations, and rights. |
| Virtue | The moral character of the person or organization. |

### Consequentialist Approach

**The Decision**

The company calculates the total impact. If a data breach occurs, it could compromise the personal records of 10 million users, causing massive collective harm, identity theft, and lawsuits. Taking the system offline causes a definitive, but contained, financial loss for a few hundred corporate clients.

**The Action**

They choose to **take the service offline immediately** because preventing a massive data breach results in the "greatest good for the greatest number" of people.

### Duty-Based Approach

**The Decision**

This framework ignores the financial math entirely. It focuses on the fundamental obligations the company has to its users. Under Kantian ethics, secretly leaving users vulnerable is a form of deception and uses the customers merely as a financial cushion to protect the company's profits.

**The Action**

They **disclose the flaw immediately and take the system offline**. They do this because lying or hiding a risk violates the universal duty to be honest and respect consumer rights, even if the financial fallout is devastating.

### Virtue Approach

**The Decision**

The company looks inward at its core values. They ask: *"What would a trustworthy, transparent, and courageous organization do?"* Covering up the flaw out of fear of financial loss or bad press represents cowardice and dishonesty.

**The Action**

**They admit the mistake**, take the system down, and fix it openly. They prioritize transparency and accountability because those are the virtues required to build long-term integrity and character, rather than just optimizing a short-term outcome.
